# 6. Conclusions and Future Work

This study has addressed the critical challenge of optimal defensive resource allocation in maritime non-traditional security environments, characterized by profound information asymmetry and stringent logistical constraints. By extending the canonical FlipIt game into a novel **Maritime Cheat-FlipIt** framework, we have formalized the adversarial dynamics where attackers exploit deceptive channels—such as false flags and camouflaged vessels—to erode defensive efficacy. To solve this high-dimensional, stochastic game, we proposed a robust decision-making architecture based on the **Rainbow DQN** algorithm, integrating prioritized experience replay and dueling networks to handle the inherent non-stationarity of the adversarial landscape.

The empirical evidence derived from our comprehensive Monte Carlo simulations substantiates the theoretical premise that deep reinforcement learning can transcend the limitations of heuristic optimization in deceptive environments. Our results demonstrate that the proposed DRL agent establishes a dominant defensive posture, achieving an **8.2%** improvement in occupation utility over traditional greedy strategies under the most severe uncertainty conditions. More significantly, the agent autonomously emerged with a **hierarchical defense doctrine**—transitioning from low-cost surveillance patrols during resource scarcity to capital-intensive naval deterrence during abundance—without any explicit rule-based programming. This emergent behavior provides a compelling validation of the algorithm’s capacity to internalize the complex trade-offs between immediate containment costs and long-term control stability, effectively solving the "friction" introduced by the deception mechanism which was quantified to degrade baseline performance by approximately 7.9%.

From a managerial perspective, this research offers actionable insights for maritime security policymakers and operational commanders. The demonstrated superiority of the dynamic, state-aware policy over static resource allocation models suggests that modern maritime defense systems must evolve from rigid, pre-planned schedules to adaptive, feedback-driven protocols. The quantifiable "Deception Cost" identified in our ablation studies underscores the imperative of integrating counter-deception intelligence modules into existing surveillance grids, as the failure to account for informational asymmetry can lead to catastrophic resource misallocation. Furthermore, the robust performance of our agent in limiting attacker success rates to near-zero levels provides a proof-of-concept for the deployment of autonomous decision-support systems in protecting critical offshore infrastructure against asymmetric threats.

While this work establishes a rigorous baseline for AI-driven maritime defense, several avenues for future inquiry warrant attention. First, our current model assumes a single defender against a single, albeit deceptive, attacker; extending this to a **multi-agent setting** with heterogeneous defenders (e.g., coalition forces) and colluding attackers would better reflect the complexity of regional security complexes. Second, the action space in our simulation is discrete; investigating **continuous control policies** using algorithms like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) could allow for more granular resource modulation, such as variable patrol velocities or continuous sensor gain adjustments. Finally, bridging the "sim-to-real" gap by incorporating high-fidelity AIS (Automatic Identification System) data streams into the training pipeline remains a critical step towards operational deployment. Nevertheless, the Maritime Cheat-FlipIt framework presented herein serves as a foundational paradigm for understanding and engineering resilience in the face of evolving maritime threats.
