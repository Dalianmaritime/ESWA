# 5. Experimental Results and Analysis

In this section, we present a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed Rainbow DQN-based defensive framework within the maritime Cheat-FlipIt game. Our experimental design adopts a rigorous $2 \times 2$ factorial structure, systematically examining the interplay between algorithmic efficacy (Deep Reinforcement Learning versus Traditional Heuristic) and environmental uncertainty (Cheat Mode versus Standard FlipIt Mode). The primary objective is to validate whether the proposed autonomous agent can transcend the limitations of myopic optimization to establish a robust Nash equilibrium approximation in environments characterized by significant information asymmetry and resource scarcity. All reported results represent the aggregation of 200 independent Monte Carlo simulation episodes to ensure statistical significance.

## 5.1 Performance Evaluation in Asymmetric Adversarial Environments

The efficacy of a defensive strategy in maritime security is fundamentally measured by its ability to maintain continuous control over critical assets while minimizing resource expenditure. As illustrated in the comparative performance metrics (Table 3) and the comprehensive radar analysis (**Fig. 1**), the proposed Rainbow DQN agent demonstrates a decisive operational superiority over the traditional greedy baseline. Specifically, under the high-uncertainty Cheat mode—which serves as the primary testbed for our hypothesis—the DRL agent achieves a mean occupation reward of **53.90**, outperforming the baseline’s **49.82** by a statistically significant margin of approximately **8.2%**. This performance gap is not merely numerical but indicative of a qualitative shift in decision-making paradigms. While the greedy strategy, constrained by its immediate payoff maximization logic, often exhausts its budget prematurely in response to feint attacks, the DRL agent exhibits a sophisticated temporal resource smoothing capability. By learning the latent value of budget conservation through the prioritized experience replay mechanism, the agent effectively neutralizes the attacker’s attempts to induce resource depletion, thereby maintaining a consistent defensive posture throughout the episode duration.

Furthermore, the robustness of the proposed framework is underscored by its suppression capability. Throughout the comprehensive evaluation phase, the DRL-driven defense successfully restricted the attacker's win rate to **0.0%**, effectively creating an impenetrable denial-of-service condition for the adversary. This contrasts sharply with the baseline approach, which, despite achieving a comparable win rate suppression, incurred significantly higher operational costs, resulting in a lower net utility. The resource efficiency analysis (**Fig. 2**) corroborates this observation, revealing that the DRL agent generates higher occupation utility per unit of budget consumed, a critical attribute for sustained maritime operations where logistical constraints are often binding.

## 5.2 Deception Dynamics and Information Asymmetry

A central theoretical contribution of this study lies in the explicit modeling of the deception channel, which fundamentally alters the payoff landscape of the classical FlipIt game. To quantify the "friction" introduced by imperfect information, we conducted an ablation study comparing system performance across Cheat and FlipIt modes (**Table 4**). The empirical evidence reveals a tangible *Deception Cost*: the introduction of the cheat mechanism precipitates a universal decline in defensive performance, with the DRL agent’s mean reward dropping from **58.50** in the transparent FlipIt mode to **53.90** in the deceptive Cheat mode. This degradation, quantifying a reward loss of approximately 4.60 units, represents the inevitable entropic penalty paid for operating under partial observability.

However, the resilience of the learning algorithm is manifested in its comparative advantage. Remarkably, the DRL agent operating under the adverse conditions of the Cheat mode (**53.90**) still outperforms the traditional agent operating under the favorable conditions of the FlipIt mode (**52.95**). This counter-intuitive finding suggests that the algorithmic superiority of deep reinforcement learning is sufficient to compensate for the informational disadvantage inherent in deceptive environments. The analysis of attack action distribution (**Fig. 3**) further elucidates the strategic drift induced by the deception mechanism. In the Cheat mode, the adversary strategically shifts towards low-cost, high-deception vectors—such as disguised fishing vessels—attempting to exploit the defender's uncertainty. In response, the DRL agent does not retreat into a passive shell but rather adopts a probabilistic interception strategy, effectively raising the cost of deception for the attacker and forcing the game equilibrium towards a state favorable to the defender.

## 5.3 Emergent Strategies and Convergence Kinetics

Beyond macroscopic performance metrics, it is imperative to dissect the micro-structural evolution of the defensive policy to understand *how* the agent solves the resource allocation puzzle. The training convergence trajectory (**Fig. 4**) delineates a three-phase learning process: an initial exploration phase characterized by high variance and suboptimal rewards, followed by a rapid exploitation phase where the agent discovers the dominant strategy, and finally a stabilization phase where the policy is fine-tuned to handle edge cases. The swift convergence—stabilizing within the first 100 episodes—validates the efficiency of the Rainbow architecture, particularly the contribution of the Dueling Network component in decoupling state value estimation from action advantage, thereby accelerating learning in states with similar action outcomes.

Perhaps the most profound insight emerges from the budget-action sensitivity analysis (**Fig. 6**), which visualizes the agent's decision preferences across different resource states. Without any explicit rule-based programming, the agent spontaneously developed a **hierarchical defense doctrine** strikingly similar to real-world naval protocols. In low-budget states (0-20 units), the heatmap shows a strong preference for *Patrol Boats*, a low-cost solution that maintains minimal presence to prevent total loss of control. Conversely, as the budget reservoir replenishes (60-80 units), the policy shifts dramatically towards capital-intensive assets like *Naval Escorts* and *Helicopter Support*. This emergent behavior indicates that the agent has successfully encoded a non-linear utility function that dynamically weighs the marginal cost of deployment against the risk of territory loss. Unlike the static heuristic of the traditional baseline, this dynamic adaptability allows the DRL agent to weather resource droughts while maximizing dominance during periods of abundance, thereby solving the core dilemma of the Cheat-FlipIt game.

## 5.4 Temporal Evolution of Defensive Strategies

To further elucidate the adaptive nature of the proposed framework, we investigate the temporal evolution of action selection probabilities throughout the learning process. The comparative analysis of action distributions between the early training phase (Episodes 0-50) and the late stabilization phase (Episodes 200-250) reveals a distinct strategic maturation trajectory (**Fig. 7**).

In the Cheat mode (**Fig. 7a**), the agent initially exhibits a quasi-uniform exploration pattern with a slight bias towards low-cost *Patrol Boats* (40%), reflecting a risk-averse posture under high uncertainty. However, as training progresses, the policy undergoes a radical restructuring. The mature agent significantly increases its reliance on *Naval Escorts* (rising from 15% to 40%) and *Platform Security* teams, while reducing *Patrol Boat* usage to a negligible level (5%). This shift indicates that the agent has learned to prioritize high-probability interception assets to counter the "fog of war" induced by the deception mechanism, effectively trading budget efficiency for higher security assurance.

Conversely, in the FlipIt mode (**Fig. 7b**), the strategic evolution follows a different path. The late-stage policy maintains a more balanced portfolio, with *Naval Escorts* (30%) and *Platform Security* (30%) sharing the defensive burden. Notably, the usage of *Helicopter Support* remains higher compared to the Cheat mode, suggesting that in environments with reliable information, the agent can afford to leverage rapid-response assets that might be too risky under deceptive conditions. This divergence in convergent strategies underscores the DRL agent's capacity to tailor its doctrine to the specific informational characteristics of the operational environment, a key requirement for autonomous maritime security systems.
