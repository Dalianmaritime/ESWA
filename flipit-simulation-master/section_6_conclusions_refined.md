# 6. Conclusions and Future Work

This study has systematically addressed the exigency of optimal defensive resource allocation within maritime non-traditional security environments, a domain historically plagued by the dual challenges of profound information asymmetry and stringent logistical constraints. By extending the canonical FlipIt game into a novel **Maritime Cheat-FlipIt** framework, we have successfully formalized the adversarial dynamics wherein attackers exploit deceptive channels—ranging from false flags to camouflaged vessels—to erode defensive efficacy. To resolve this high-dimensional, stochastic game, we proposed and validated a robust decision-making architecture founded on the **Rainbow DQN** algorithm, which integrates prioritized experience replay and dueling networks to navigate the inherent non-stationarity of the adversarial landscape.

The empirical evidence derived from our rigorous Monte Carlo simulations substantiates the theoretical premise that deep reinforcement learning can fundamentally transcend the limitations of heuristic optimization in deceptive environments. Our results demonstrate that the proposed DRL agent establishes a dominant defensive posture, achieving an **8.2%** improvement in occupation utility over traditional greedy strategies under the most severe conditions of uncertainty. More significantly, the agent autonomously emerged with a **hierarchical defense doctrine**—transitioning from low-cost surveillance patrols during periods of resource scarcity to capital-intensive naval deterrence during phases of abundance—without any explicit rule-based programming. This emergent behavior provides a compelling validation of the algorithm’s capacity to internalize the complex trade-offs between immediate containment costs and long-term control stability, effectively mitigating the "friction" introduced by the deception mechanism, which our ablation studies quantified as a baseline performance degradation of approximately 7.9%.

From a theoretical and managerial perspective, this research bridges the chasm between abstract game-theoretic equilibrium and actionable maritime security protocols. The demonstrated superiority of the dynamic, state-aware policy over static resource allocation models suggests that modern maritime defense systems must evolve from rigid, pre-planned schedules to adaptive, feedback-driven paradigms. The quantifiable "Deception Cost" identified in this study underscores the imperative of integrating counter-deception intelligence modules into existing surveillance grids, as the failure to account for informational asymmetry can lead to catastrophic resource misallocation. Furthermore, the robust performance of our agent in limiting attacker success rates to near-zero levels serves as a proof-of-concept for the deployment of autonomous decision-support systems in safeguarding critical offshore infrastructure against asymmetric threats.

While this work establishes a rigorous baseline for AI-driven maritime defense, several avenues for future inquiry warrant attention to further enhance ecological validity. First, our current model postulates a single defender against a single, albeit deceptive, attacker; extending this framework to a **multi-agent setting** with heterogeneous defenders (e.g., coalition forces) and colluding attackers would better reflect the complexity of regional security complexes. Second, the action space in our simulation is currently discrete; investigating **continuous control policies** using algorithms such as Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) could facilitate more granular resource modulation, such as variable patrol velocities or continuous sensor gain adjustments. Finally, bridging the "sim-to-real" gap by incorporating high-fidelity AIS (Automatic Identification System) data streams into the training pipeline remains a critical step towards operational deployment. Nevertheless, the Maritime Cheat-FlipIt framework presented herein serves as a foundational paradigm for understanding and engineering resilience in the face of evolving maritime threats.
