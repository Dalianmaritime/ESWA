2.  Literature Review

﹛﹛Maritime security defense has long been a focal point of transportation research, evolving from static risk assessments to dynamic adversarial modeling. This section synthesizes existing research across three interconnected domains:  maritime risk assessment,  game-theoretic security models, and deep reinforcement learning in adversarial contexts.  We critically analyze the progression of these methodologies to highlight the speci?c gapnamely, the lack of adaptive defense mechanisms against deceptive threatsthat this study addresses.
2.1.  From Static Risk
﹛﹛Assessment to Dynamic Adversarial ModelingEarly research in maritime security primarily re- lied on quantitative risk assessment (QRA) frameworks.  Methodologies such as Bayesian Networks (BNs) and Fuzzy Analytical Hierarchy Process  (AHP) have been widely adopted to evaluate the vulnerability of o?shore assets and shipping routes.  For instance, Yang et al.  (2018) utilized a fuzzy Bayesian network to assess risk factors in maritime supply chains, providing a robust framework for identifying critical vulnerabilities under static conditions.   Similarly,  Zhang and Thai  (2016) proposed a vulnerability model for port infrastructure, emphasizing the cascading e?ects of secu- rity breaches.While these approaches provide a foundational understanding of security postures, they inherently assume a static environment where threat probabilities remain constant or follow a ?xed distribution.  This assumption, however, fails to capture the intelligent and adaptive na- ture of modern adversaries, such as pirates and terrorists, who dynamically alter their tactics in response to defense measures.  As noted by limitations in recent reviews, static models cannot ac- count for the continuous interaction between attackers and defenders, necessitating a shift towards game-theoretic approaches that explicitly model this adversarial interplay.

2.2.   The FlipIt Game and Control-Theoretic Security
﹛﹛To address the dynamism of persistent threats, game theoryspeci?cally the "FlipIt" game intro- duced by van Dijk et al.  (2013)has emerged as a powerful paradigm.  Unlike one-shot Stackelberg security games often used in patrol planning, FlipIt models a continuous, stealthy takeover game where players compete for control of a resource over time. This structure is particularly analogous to maritime platform defense, where control can be lost (e.g., via a stealthy boarding) without im- mediate detection.Recent extensions of FlipIt have explored various dimensions of the game. Laszka et al.  (2014) analyzed FlipIt under resource constraints, proving that periodic strategies are often suboptimal when costs vary.   Further work by Bowers et al.   (2012) applied similar concepts to defending against Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs) in cyber-physical systems.  However, a crit- ical limitation persists in this body of literature:  standard FlipIt models typically assume perfect veri?cation, meaning that once a defender "checks" or "defends," they gain accurate knowledge of the system state.  In the maritime domain, this assumption is ?awed.  Adversaries frequently em- ploy deceptionsuch as AIS spoo?ng or false distress signalscreating information asymmetry where a defenders action does not guarantee an accurate assessment of control.  The inability of classical FlipIt models to incorporate this deceptive dimension represents a signi?cant theoretical gap.
2.3.   Deception in Adversarial Interactions
﹛﹛The role of deception in security games has garnered increasing attention.   Current research often utilizes signaling games or hypergames to model scenarios where players act based on di?er- ent perceptions of the game state.  Pawlick et al.   (2019) provided a comprehensive taxonomy of deception in cyber-physical systems, categorizing attacks based on information manipulation.  In the maritime context, determining the veracity of incoming signals is crucial.  Zhuang et al.  (2010) modeled deception in varying security contexts, demonstrating that the presence of secrecy and deception fundamentally alters equilibrium strategies,  often favoring attackers who can success- fully manipulate the defenders beliefs.Despite these theoretical advancements, solving equilibrium strategies for games involving continuous time, resource constraints, and deceptive actions is com- putationally intractable using traditional analytical methods. As the complexity of the state space increasesintroduced by the stochastic nature of deception and the continuous accumulation of "oc- cupation rewards"?nding a closed-form solution becomes infeasible.  This computational bottleneck paves the way for learning-based approaches.
2.4.   Deep Reinforcement Learning for  Complex Security  Games
﹛﹛The advent of Deep Reinforcement Learning  (DRL) has provided new tools for solving high- dimensional, dynamic games.  Since the breakthrough of Deep Q-Networks (DQN) by Mnih et al. (2015), DRL has been successfully applied to complex decision-making problems where an agent must learn from interaction rather than pre-programmed rules.  In the realm of transportation and security, Wang et al.  (2023) demonstrated the e?cacy of DRL in optimizing tra?c signal control under  dynamic  ?ow  conditions,  while  Nguyen  and  Reddi  (2021)  surveyed  DRL  applications  in cyber security, highlighting its superiority in detecting novel attack patterns compared to heuristic

﹛methods.However, the application of DRL to maritime non-traditional security remains nascent. Most existing DRL applications in the maritime sector focus on autonomous navigation or collision avoidance, rather than adversarial defense. Furthermore, standard DQN algorithms often struggle with the  speci?c  challenges  of security  games,  such  as  overestimation  of Q-values  in  stochastic environments.  This necessitates the use of advanced variants, such as Rainbow DQN  (Hessel et al.,  2018), which combines multiple improvements to enhance stability and sample e?ciency.2.5 SummaryIn conclusion, while the literature has progressed from static risk analysis to dynamic game theory, there remains a distinct lack of frameworks that simultaneously address continuous- time contention, deceptive adversarial tactics, and adaptive strategy learning within the maritime domain.   This study bridges these gaps by proposing the Cheat-FlipIt model to mathematically formalize deceptive takeovers and employing a Rainbow DQN-based agent to computationally solve for robust defense strategies, thereby integrating game-theoretic rigor with the adaptive power of deep learning.

5.4.   Emergent Strategies and Convergence Kinetics
﹛﹛Beyond macroscopic performance metrics, it is imperative to dissect the micro-structural evo- lution of the defensive policy to understand how the agent solves the resource allocation puzzle. The training convergence trajectory (Fig.  4) delineates a three-phase learning process:  an initial exploration phase characterized by high variance and suboptimal rewards, followed by a rapid ex- ploitation phase where the agent discovers the dominant strategy, and ?nally a stabilization phase where the policy is ?ne-tuned to handle edge cases.  The swift convergencestabilizing within the ?rst 100 episodesvalidates the e?ciency of the Rainbow architecture, particularly the contribution of the Dueling Network component in decoupling state value estimation from action advantage, thereby accelerating learning in states with similar action outcomes.
Perhaps the most profound insight emerges from the budget-action sensitivity analysis  (Fig.
﹛6), which visualizes the agent＊s decision preferences across di?erent resource states.  Without any explicit rule-based programming, the agent spontaneously developed a hierarchical defense doctrine strikingly similar to real-world naval protocols.   In low-budget states  (0-20 units), the heatmap shows a strong preference for Patrol Boats, a low-cost solution that maintains minimal presence to prevent total loss of control. Conversely, as the budget reservoir replenishes (60-80 units), the policy shifts dramatically towards capital-intensive assets like Naval Escorts and Helicopter Support.  This emergent behavior indicates that the agent has successfully encoded a non-linear utility function that dynamically weighs the marginal cost of deployment against the risk of territory loss.  Unlike the static heuristic of the traditional baseline,  this dynamic adaptability allows the DRL agent to weather resource droughts while maximizing dominance during periods of abundance, thereby solving the core dilemma of the Cheat-FlipIt game.
